6 Multivariate Normal Distribution

Most of the inferential statistical methods, such as \(t\) tests, one-way ANOVA, are based on the normality assumption in univariate analysis. Similarly, some multivariate analysis techniques are based on the assumption that the data are from a multivariate normal distribution.

The univariate density function of normal distribution with mean \(\mu\) and standard deviation \(\sigma\) is \[\begin{equation} \label{eq:uninormal} f(x)=\frac{1}{\sqrt{2\pi}\sigma}e^{-\frac{(x-\mu)^2}{2\sigma^2}}, -\infty<x<\infty \end{equation}\]

The term \[ \left(\frac{x-\mu}{\sigma}\right)^2=(x-\mu)(\sigma^2)^{-1}(x-\mu) \] in the exponent of the univariate normal density function measures the squared distance between \(x\) and \(\mu\) in standard deviation units. This can be generalized in vectors for multivariate cases as \[ (\mathbf{x}-\gvec{\mu})^{T}\gvec{\Sigma}^{-1} (\mathbf{x}-\gvec{\mu}) \] where \(\gvec{\mu}\) and \(\mathbf{\Sigma}\) are the mean vector and variance-covariance matrix. It can be shown that the \(p\)-dimensional multivariate normal density function is \[\begin{equation} \label{eq:multinormal} f(\mathbf{x})=\frac{1}{(2\pi)^{p/2}|\mathbf{\Sigma}|^{1/2}}\exp \left\{-\frac{(\mathbf{x}-\gvec{\mu})^{T}\gvec{\Sigma}^{-1} (\mathbf{x}-\gvec{\mu})}{2}\right\}. \end{equation}\]

As the constant \(\frac{1}{\sqrt{2\pi}\sigma}\) is the normalizing constant such that the area under the density curve given in Equation (\(\ref{eq:uninormal}\)) is 1, the constant \(\frac{1}{(2\pi)^{p/2}|\mathbf{\Sigma}|^{1/2}}\) is the normalizing constant ensuring that the volume under the surface defined in Equation (\(\ref{eq:multinormal}\)) is 1.

6.2 Bivariate Normal Distribution

It can be shown that when \(p=2\), we have the bivariate normal density \[\begin{equation} \label{eq:denbivariate} f(x_1, x_2)=\frac{1}{2\pi\sqrt{\sigma_{11}\sigma_{22}(1-\rho_{12}^2)}}\exp\left\{-\frac{1}{2(1-\rho_{12}^2)}\left[\left(\frac{x_1-\mu_1}{\sqrt{\sigma_{11}}}\right)^2+\left(\frac{x_2-\mu_2}{\sqrt{\sigma_{22}}}\right)^2-2\rho_{12}\left(\frac{x_1-\mu_1}{\sqrt{\sigma_{11}}}\right)\left(\frac{x_2-\mu_2}{\sqrt{\sigma_{22}}}\right)\right]\right\} \end{equation}\] where \(\mu_1=E(X_1), \mu_2=E(X_2), \sigma_{11}=Var(X_1), \sigma_{22}=Var(X_2), \rho_{12}=\frac{\sigma_{12}}{\sqrt{\sigma_{11}\sigma_{22}}}.\)

6.3 Contour of Multivariate Normal Distribution

Definition

Contour at level \(c_0^2\) is the collection of all points of \(\myvec{x}\) such that \(f(\myvec{x})=c_0^2\). Since \[ f(\mathbf{x})=\frac{1}{(2\pi)^{p/2}|\gvec{\Sigma}|^{1/2}}\exp \left\{-\frac{(\mathbf{x}-\gvec{\mu})^{T}\gvec{\Sigma}^{-1} (\mathbf{x}-\gvec{\mu})}{2}\right\}=c_0^2 \Longrightarrow (\mathbf{x}-\gvec{\mu})^{T}\gvec{\Sigma}^{-1} (\mathbf{x}-\gvec{\mu})=c^2 \] a \((1-\alpha)\times 100\%\) contour is the collection of points \(\myvec{x}\) such that \[ P((\mathbf{x}-\gvec{\mu})^{T}\gvec{\Sigma}^{-1} (\mathbf{x}-\gvec{\mu})\le c^2)=1-\alpha. \] Since \((\mathbf{x}-\gvec{\mu})^{T}\gvec{\Sigma}^{-1} (\mathbf{x}-\gvec{\mu})\sim \chi^2_{p}\), we get \(c^2=\chi^2_{p, \alpha}\). For example, a 95% contour for a bivariate normal distribution is the collection of all points \(\myvec{x}\) such that \[ (\mathbf{x}-\gvec{\mu})^{T}\gvec{\Sigma}^{-1} (\mathbf{x}-\gvec{\mu})\le \chi^2_{2, 0.05}=5.991 \]

Example: Contour of Multivariate Normal Distribution

Theorem

The contour \((\mathbf{x}-\gvec{\mu})^{T}\gvec{\Sigma}^{-1} (\mathbf{x}-\gvec{\mu})\le c^2\) is an ellipsoids centered at \(\gvec{\mu}\) and have axes \(\pm c\sqrt{\lambda_i}\mathbf{e}_i\), where \(\lambda_i\) and \(\mathbf{e}_i\) are the eigenvalues and corresponding unit eigenvectors of the variance-covariance matrix \(\gvec{\Sigma}\).
